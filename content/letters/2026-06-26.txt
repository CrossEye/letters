Title: The Monster Under the Math 
Date: 2026-06-26 
Topics: Andover, RHAM, Jeff Maguire
People: Jeff Maguire
TeaserParagraphs: 2

At a recent public meeting, Andover's First Selectman warned residents about a
"monster number": he said it costs about $34,000 a year to send each Andover
child to RHAM, and suggested that I, as a member of the RHAM Board of Education,
should be focusing on it.

I am glad to focus on what RHAM costs. I am also glad to turn on the light,
because this particular monster does not survive it.

The arithmetic is something any resident can do. RHAM's entire budget this year
is $32,620,072, and the district enrolls 1,078 students. Divide the first by the
second and you get $30,261 per student. That figure includes everything:
salaries, buildings, buses, and debt service, spread across every child in the
district. It is the largest the per-student number can possibly be. The First
Selectman's $34,000 exceeds even that maximum, which is another way of saying it
cannot be real.

You reach the same place doing it Andover-only. Our town's RHAM assessment this
year is $4,237,348, and we send 140 students. Dividing one by the other gives
the same figure, give or take a rounding difference. The two methods agree
because each town's bill is set in proportion to its enrollment.

The state's official measure, which Connecticut calculates the same way for
every district, is lower still: $27,181 per pupil in operating cost. By that
measure, only two of the eight comparable regional districts in the state spend
less per pupil than RHAM.

None of this means RHAM is cheap, and I will not pretend otherwise. Its
per-pupil cost has climbed sharply over the past decade. But the climb is mostly
because enrollment fell by more than a third, not because spending grew. RHAM's
total budget rose more slowly than inflation. I lay all of this out, with
primary sources, in a report at andoverct.info/reports/rham/per-pupil.

Some will ask why I did not say this while the budget was before the voters. I
sit on the RHAM board, and I make a deliberate choice to stay out of the public
argument over the budget, in either direction. I do not believe anyone holding a
seat should put a thumb on the scale of a live referendum. However, the vote is
settled now; I'm free to speak.

We can have a real argument about school spending. We cannot have it with
imaginary monsters, imaginary numbers.
