To my Andover neighbors who voted No:

I understand the impulse. Budgets keep climbing, paychecks and pensions don't; a referendum is one of the few places we can plant a foot and say "enough."  Most of those No votes were cast in good faith.  But let's discuss what the vote bought, and what it cost.

Between the first budget and the last, the school budget came down by $224,261. Set aside $5,000 for the extra referenda, spread it across 3,150 of us, and it comes to $69.61 each. Call it seventy bucks. Real money, yes. But the bill did not land on us. It landed on the children.

To close the gap, the Board had to cut:

* Two paraprofessional aides, the extra hands for the kids who need a boost to keep up. Gone.

* The Spanish program. Not Cervantes yet, but the second most spoken language in the country, s=during the years a young ear picks it up most easily. Adios, Spanish.

* The stipends for teachers who stay past the bell: curriculum work, the robotics coach, the yearbook, the Jazz Club. Gone, or after-hours only.

* And the library. They eliminated the librarian, with nothing left even for new books. The room will still be there, and the shelves. The person who turns it into something a child loves will not.

Let me say that one more time, because it is the one I cannot get past. We cut the school library.

Let me tell you about Mrs. Hatch. At Great Plains Elementary in the early '70s, I knew the best part of school was the playground. But only slightly behind it was this mystical, magical room called The Library. Mrs. Hatch ruled the roost there. She read us stories when we were small; as we grew she taught us to read them aloud to the littler ones. She showed us how to research, how to branch out and try new kinds of books. She taught us to care for a book, and never to abuse one. And she taught at least one little boy a love for reading that has lasted to this day. Mrs. Hatch may have only been 50, but to a third-grader that was as old as the hills, with the wisdom to match.

What we decided is that our own children don't need a Mrs. Hatch.  I think we are terribly wrong.  In a faster, more distracted world than the one I grew up in, there is nothing a child needs more than someone to hand them the right book at the right moment.

I keep coming back to those seventy dollars. I don't believe any of us, with a child in front of us, would trade that child's library for seventy dollars. And the fight isn't over: the same needs return next year, only now a year of a child's reading life has already been spent.

When you spend your seventy dollars, spare a thought for what it cost.
