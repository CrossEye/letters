Members of RHAM's Board of Education have seen a recent increase in communications from the community asking the Board to investigate school matters. These writers are often surprised to receive no response from the Board. From now on, the Board will make every effort to respond more consistently, but the community should understand why many of those responses may do little more than refer concerns to the administration.

The reason is simple: many of these matters fall outside the authority of a school board.

While Board of Education members are elected by the community and accountable to it, they are also agents of the state, responsible for ensuring the appropriate education of our children. Their responsibilities are limited and defined. They have three primary duties: hiring and evaluating the Superintendent, adopting an annual budget, and setting district policy. There is a fourth—ideally rare—role: serving as the final arbiter in unresolved disputes between the administration and students or staff. That is the full scope of the Board’s authority.

Individual members do not have independent authority. The Board can act only as a body, and only during duly called meetings. Teachers manage classrooms, principals manage schools, and the Superintendent manages the district. The Board does not run day-to-day operations. Instead, it oversees one employee—the Superintendent—while maintaining responsibility for district policy and the overall budget.

Accordingly, community members with concerns about classroom or extracurricular matters should begin by speaking with the teacher, coach or advisor. If the issue is not resolved, they should contact the principal or athletic director, and then, if necessary, the Superintendent. When such concerns are brought directly to the Board of Education, the Board’s role is limited to directing the Superintendent to address the issue through the appropriate administrative channels.

We welcome and cherish your feedback. But please understand that our response is often constrained by state and federal law.
