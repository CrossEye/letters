To my neighbors who voted No:

I understand the impulse. Budgets keep climbing, paychecks and pensions do not, and a town referendum is one of the few places an ordinary person gets to plant a foot and say "enough." I don't doubt that most of those No votes were cast in good faith, by people who love this town. So let me be straight with you about what the vote bought, and what it cost.

First, what it saved. Between the first budget and the last, the school budget came down by $224,261. Set aside about $5,000 for the two extra referenda we had to run to get here. Spread the remainder across all 3,150 of us, and it comes to $69.61 a person. Call it seventy bucks. That is real money, and I won't pretend it isn't. But I want you to know what those seventy dollars cost, because the bill did not land on us. It landed on the children.

To close the gap, the Board of Education had to cut:

  * Two paraprofessional aides, the extra hands for the kids who need a little more to keep up. Gone.

  * The Spanish program. Our kids weren't reading Cervantes yet, but they were hearing the second most spoken language in this country, in the years when a young ear picks up a language most easily. Adiós, Spanish.

  * The stipends for the teachers who stay past the bell for our kids: curriculum work, the robotics coach, the yearbook, the Jazz Club. The Superintendent will likely stitch together a grant to keep some of it alive, but only as after-hours work, no longer part of the school day.

  * And the library. They eliminated the librarian, with nothing left even for new books. The room will still be there. The shelves will still be there. The person who turns all of it into something a child loves will not.

Let me say that part again, because it is the one I cannot get past. We cut the school library.

Let me tell you about Mrs. Hatch. At Great Plains Elementary School in the early '70s, like every other kid, I knew the best part of school was the playground. But only slightly behind it was this mystical, magical room called The Library. Mrs. Hatch ruled the roost there for my whole elementary school. She read us stories when we were small, and as we grew she taught us to read them aloud to the little ones coming up behind us. She showed us how to research, how to branch out and try new kinds of books. She taught us how to care for a book, and to never, ever abuse one. And she taught at least one little boy a love for reading that has lasted to this day. Mrs. Hatch may have only been 50, but to a third-grader that was as old as the hills, with the wisdom to match.

What we decided is that our own children don't need a Mrs. Hatch. I think we are terribly wrong about that. In a faster, noisier, more distracted world than the one I grew up in, there is hardly anything a child needs more than someone to hand them the right book at the right moment, and the love of reading that comes with it.

So I keep coming back to those seventy dollars. I don't believe that any of us, asked the question plainly, with a child in front of us, would trade that child's library for seventy dollars. I don't think that is who Andover is. And the hardest part is that the fight isn't even over. The same needs come back next year, only now a year of a child's reading life has already been spent.

When you spend your seventy dollars, I hope you will spare a thought for what it cost.
