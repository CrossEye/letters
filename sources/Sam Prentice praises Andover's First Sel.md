Sam Prentice praises Andover's First Selectman for "due diligence" on the
preschool. Then he writes a letter that demonstrates he hasn't done any himself.
 
Due diligence would have meant reading the public record before declaring it a
bad investment. The audit. The Comptroller's payment ledger. The Board of
Education's monthly financial reports. The collective bargaining agreements. The
school's published enrollment policies. All public. All on the internet.
 
I read them. They tell a consistent story across every independent source:
 
The preschool brings in about $436,000 a year in tuition and state grants. It
costs about $528,000 a year fully loaded, including the employee benefits the
BOE doesn't allocate at the program level. That's a $92,000 operating gap —
about $5.50 per Andover household per month.
 
But the district is required by federal law (IDEA Part B Section 619) and state
law (CGS §10-76d) to provide special education to children ages 3 to 6 with
disabilities, whether the preschool exists or not. Meeting that obligation by
outplacement or a standalone special-ed program would cost the town around
$200,000 a year — roughly twice what the current preschool costs in net.
 
So the preschool *saves* Andover taxpayers about $108,000 a year.
 
Mr. Prentice complains the Superintendent is hiding "an undisclosed or
overlooked source of money." There is no such source. The one figure not
publicly broken out is the employee benefits allocation — and the Superintendent
is not allowed to publish it. Federal regulations under FERPA (34 CFR §99.3) and
HIPAA (45 CFR §164.504(f)) both prohibit her from disclosing aggregate
information about a group as small as eight staff when their roles can be linked
to specific student needs. That's not her opinion. It's the law. She would be
derelict to do otherwise. The First Selectman has been told this repeatedly. He
keeps demanding it anyway, and his admirers keep treating her refusal as
obstruction.
 
Mr. Prentice's letter accuses the Superintendent of having "no regard
whatsoever" for taxpayers and praises the First Selectman as a steward of our
resources. The actual stewardship record: the Superintendent runs a program that
saves the town six figures a year. The First Selectman put out a flyer
suggesting the preschool might be costing us money — without checking. That's
form. The numbers above are substance.

I would humbly suggest that *anyone* writing about the budget might read it
